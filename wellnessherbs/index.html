In general, herbs are a widely distributed and widespread group of plants with savory or aromatic properties that are used for flavoring and garnishing food, for medicinal purposes, or for fragrances. The term “herb” refers to the leafy, flowering or root parts of a plant. Culinary herbs are distinguished from vegetables in that, like spices, they are used in small amounts and provide flavor rather than substance to food. Some herbs can be infused in boiling water to make herbal teas. Typically, the leaves, either fresh or dried, flowers or seeds are used. Herbal teas tend to be made from aromatic herbs. Common examples include chamomile tea or peppermint tea, which are often used as a source of relaxation. Herbs were used in prehistoric medicine. As far back as 5000 BCE, evidence that Sumerians used herbs in medicine was inscribed on cuneiform. Herbs have long been used as the basis of traditional Chinese herbal medicine, with usage dating as far back as the first century CE. Modern pharmaceuticals had their origins in crude herbal medicines, and to this day, some drugs are still extracted as fractionate/isolate compounds from raw herbs and then purified to meet pharmaceutical standards.

Our Materia Medica page includes information about common herbs such as where its grown, a description, pictures, common usage, preparation, and precautionary statements. All herbs must be treated with respect. Many are powerful and quite potent in small doses. Herbs may also have drug interactions with pharmaceutical drugs. The information provided on this site is for educational purposes only and should not be used to diagnose or treat any illness or medical condition without consulting a medical doctor.
